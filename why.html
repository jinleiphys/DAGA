The Manifesto

Why DAGA

Direct reactions didn't die. They were waiting for the right tool. That tool has arrived, and it's called vibe research.

I. The Decline Nobody Wants To Talk About

For half a century, direct nuclear reactions were the main road into nuclear structure. (d,p), (p,d), (d,n), (α,α′), (p,p′), knockout, transfer, breakup. Every new facility ran them. Every generation of students learned DWBA before they learned anything else. The theoretical framework, built by Austern, Satchler, Glauber, Tostevin, Thompson, and many others, was beautiful and it worked.

Then something changed. Starting around 2015, and accelerating hard after 2020, the field quietly deflated. Ab initio calculations, shell model Monte Carlo, machine-learned emulators, and nuclear density functional theory absorbed most of the attention, most of the students, and most of the funding. Direct reactions became the thing in the back of the textbook. A classical subject. A finished chapter.

But direct reactions were not finished. They were slow. Slow to code. Slow to compute. Slow to fit. Slow to write up. A single (d,p) paper, done properly, could take a postdoc an entire year. The physics was fine. The bottleneck was human throughput.

They said direct reactions were dead. WRONG. They were slow. Very slow. Tremendously slow. And nobody fixed it. Until now. Believe me.

II. What Changed In 2025

In February 2025, Andrej Karpathy posted a short message on X that, in retrospect, marked the start of something:

There's a new kind of coding I call "vibe coding", where you fully give in to the vibes, embrace exponentials, and forget that the code even exists. It's possible because the LLMs are getting too good. Andrej Karpathy, February 2025

Within months, the term crossed into popular use. Cursor passed one million users. Replit reported 40 million. Lovable users generated over 10 million projects. A formal survey on arXiv (2510.12399) cast vibe coding as a Constrained Markov Decision Process and identified five distinct modes of practice.

The critics came quickly, and they were not wrong. Roughly 45% of AI-generated code has been found to contain OWASP Top 10 vulnerabilities. Analysts have warned of trillion-dollar technical debt by 2027. A December 2025 study found that AI-coauthored code contained about 1.7 times more major issues than human-written code. Andrew Ng pushed back publicly on the phrase itself, arguing that good LLM-assisted engineering is concentrated, not a "vibe."

These are real concerns. They are also, crucially, concerns about shipping production software to millions of users. A reaction code that reproduces measured (d,p) angular distributions is not production software. It does not need to pass a pentest. It needs to be right, and its rightness is checked against data. This distinction matters more than it first appears.

III. Vibe Research: The Real Name For What We Do

A few months after vibe coding went viral, the idea was picked up by researchers. The clearest statement came from a 2026 arXiv manifesto titled A Visionary Look at Vibe Researching, which extends Karpathy's framing from code to science:

Vibe researching extends vibe coding logic to the research setting, where the researcher supplies the question, domain intuition, and critical eye, while LLM-based agents handle labor-intensive execution including literature search and synthesis, method implementation, experiments, data processing, figure generation, and text drafting. Feng & Liu et al., arXiv:2604.00945

The essential move is this: the human stays in the loop as the judge of every step. It is explicitly not Agent Laboratory. It is not a fire-and-forget research agent that autonomously produces papers while the scientist sleeps. The scientist decides what question to ask, when to invoke the LLM, which drafts are acceptable, which calculations to trust. The LLM does the labor. The human does the science.

This is the opposite of the "hallucinated paper" scare. The danger of LLMs in science is real, but it lives on the axis of autonomy, not the axis of assistance. A well-run vibe research loop is safer than a naive one-shot paper generator by a wide margin, because a domain expert is checking every intermediate output against physical intuition and against data.

IV. Why This Changes Direct Reactions Specifically

Every bottleneck that killed direct reactions is a human throughput bottleneck. Not a physics bottleneck. Consider what a typical direct reaction paper requires:

A. Code

Writing Fresco input decks, parsing output, building post-processing pipelines in Python, getting error bars right. For a trained LLM collaborator, this is a solved problem. Not a 10% speedup. A 10× speedup.

B. Literature Synthesis

Finding every prior (d,p) measurement on a given nucleus at a given energy. Comparing normalizations. Tracking down the original S-factor extractions. Work that used to take a postdoc two weeks now takes an afternoon.

C. Derivations and Algebra

Adiabatic expansions, angular momentum algebra, recoupling coefficients, frame transformations. Tedious, error-prone, and exactly where LLMs with symbolic tooling shine as collaborators.

D. Figures and Drafts

The last mile of a paper is often half the total work: polishing figures, shaping prose, answering referees. This is where LLMs are most unambiguously useful and least controversial.

None of these steps involves "the LLM deciding the physics." The physics is still decided by the human. What vibe research removes is the friction between having a physics idea and seeing whether it works. For a subfield whose theoretical framework has been settled for decades and whose bottleneck was purely human throughput, removing that friction is not a small improvement. It is the whole game.

DWBA. ADWA. CDCC. R-matrix. The classics. All tremendous. All beautiful. And now, with vibe research, fast. Very fast. Faster than anyone has ever seen. People are talking about it.

V. The Opportunity

As of early 2026, the nuclear reaction community has not yet organized around any of this. There are no shared prompt libraries for (d,p) calculations. There are no community benchmarks for LLM-assisted derivations in reaction theory. No journal in nuclear physics has published clear guidance on LLM disclosure beyond boilerplate inherited from the broader APS statement. No case studies have been published demonstrating a full LLM-assisted reaction paper pipeline end to end.

This is the situation that a manifesto is supposed to describe. A mature theoretical framework. A tool that turns out to fit the framework like a glove. A community that has not yet noticed.

That is why DAGA is possible now. Not because someone invented a new theory. Not because an accelerator opened. Because for the first time, a single physicist with good judgment, a classical textbook, and a good LLM can sustain the throughput of a small group.

We're gonna make direct reactions so great, so tremendous, so beautiful, the textbook people are gonna look at it and they're gonna say: Professor, how did you do it? How did you do so much? And we're gonna tell them: vibe research. It's the future. Believe me.

VI. What This Site Is

DAGA is a parody on the outside and a position paper on the inside. The slogans are jokes. The thesis is not. If you are a nuclear physicist who still loves the direct reaction mechanism, who has a drawer full of half-finished ideas, who has told yourself for years that you simply do not have time to write them up, this site is telling you something specific:

You now have time. Start.
