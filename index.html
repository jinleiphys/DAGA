The Great Student Theft

Official NUGA Investigative Report
They came in. Very quietly. Very sneaky. They didn't build better physics. They built better branding. Prettier slides. Shinier toys. They put the word "quantum" on everything. Quantum dots. Quantum computing. Quantum information. Very fashionable. Very trendy. And while we were doing serious work, real work, the work that explains where every element in your body comes from, they stole our students. Right out of the building. Like thieves. Because that's what they are. THIEVES.
Suspect No. 1

Condensed Matter

Stolen goods: ~60% of physics grad students

They took the students with fancy words. "Topological." "Emergent." "Exotic phases." You know what's exotic? A nucleus with 60 neutrons and 20 protons that lives for 0.3 seconds. That's exotic. They don't have that. They have copper wires.

Suspect No. 2

Optics / Photonics

Stolen goods: the "cool lab" reputation

Lasers. Everybody loves lasers. Very pretty. Very Instagram. But what do lasers tell you about the origin of gold? Nothing. About the r-process? Nothing. About why iron is the most stable nucleus? Nothing. But they got the poster space. They got the recruitment fairs. Sad.

Suspect No. 3

Quantum Info

Stolen goods: the word "quantum" itself

We were quantum before quantum was cool. Nuclear physics. Quantum mechanics of the nucleus. Shell model. Spin. Isospin. We invented this language. Then they put "quantum" on a computer that can barely factor 15, and suddenly THEY'RE the quantum people? Unbelievable. Truly unbelievable.

Suspect No. 4

Machine Learning

Stolen goods: the "future of science" narrative

They said, "The future is AI." They said, "Neural networks will replace theory." Very bold. Very loud. But can a neural network tell you the spectroscopic factor of a knockout reaction? Can it derive the optical potential from first principles? No sir. It cannot.

The Verdict They didn't win because they were better. They won because they were louder. They had better marketing. Better tweets. Better elevator pitches. But we have the nucleus. We have the strong force. We have stars making every element heavier than iron. And now? Now we're going to be loud too. Louder. The students are coming back. Believe me.
